Cases in German

German has three grammatical cases: nominative, accusative, and dative. These cases affect the form of articles and pronouns.

Case Masculine Feminine Neuter Plural
Nominative der die das die
Accusative den die das die
Dative dem der dem den
Case Masculine Feminine Neuter Plural
Nominative Der Mann ist nett. (The man is nice.) Die Frau liest ein Buch. (The woman is reading a book.) Das Kind spielt. (The child is playing.) Die Kinder spielen im Garten. (The children are playing in the garden.)
Accusative Ich sehe den Mann. (I see the man.) Er hat die Tasche. (He has the bag.) Wir kaufen das Auto. (We are buying the car.) Ich sehe die Kinder im Garten. (I see the children in the garden.)
Dative Ich gebe dem Mann das Buch. (I give the book to the man.) Er hilft der Frau. (He helps the woman.) Wir schicken dem Kind eine Nachricht. (We send the child a message.) Ich gebe den Kinder eind Geschenk. (I give the book to the man.)

Modal Verbs in German

Modal verbs express necessity, possibility, permission, or desire.

They are often followed by an infinitive verb at the end of the sentence.

In negative sentences with modal verbs, "nicht" is placed after the modal verb.

These verbs are often irregular, and their conjugation changes depending on the subject.
